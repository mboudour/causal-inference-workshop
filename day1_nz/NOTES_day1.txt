- Average stance (Republican): ≈ 0.043

10. Interpretation
- Republicans have higher measured stance than Democrats
- The magnitude is small compared to Part A
- The outcome is now text-derived, not arbitrary

11. Key message
- The outcome is constructed, not observed
- Measurement introduces structure and assumptions
- Different measurement → different causal conclusions

DAY 1 — PART C (Causal Adjustment & Diagnostics)
--------------------------------------------------

12. Setup
- Same dataset: Hein Bound 111, 8,000 Congressional speeches
- Outcome Y = text length (Part A proxy), Ỹ = LLM stance (Part B proxy)
- Treatment D = party (Republican = 1)
- Covariate X = year (categorical)

13. Naive estimator decomposition
- The naive difference E[Y|D=1] - E[Y|D=0] is decomposed as:
    ATT  +  Selection Bias
  where:
    ATT          = E[Y(1)|D=1] - E[Y(0)|D=1]
    Selection bias = E[Y(0)|D=1] - E[Y(0)|D=0]
- Selection bias is non-zero when treated and control units differ
  systematically in their baseline outcomes

14. Propensity score estimation & overlap check
- Propensity score: P(D=1 | X=year), estimated via logistic regression
- Overlap (positivity) check: verify that 0 < P(D=1|X) < 1 for all X
- If propensity scores are bounded away from 0 and 1, the overlap
  assumption is satisfied and identification proceeds

15. Adjustment formula (G-formula)
- Under ignorability and overlap, the ATE is identified as:
    τ = E_X[ E[Y|D=1,X] - E[Y|D=0,X] ]
- Implementation: fit OLS(Y ~ D + year_factor), predict under D=1 and D=0
  for all units, then average the difference
- This is the regression-based implementation of the adjustment formula

16. Inverse Probability Weighting (IPW)
- Alternative implementation of the adjustment formula via propensity scores
- Reweights treated and control units to make them comparable:
    ATE_IPW = E[D·Y / P(D=1|X)] - E[(1-D)·Y / P(D=0|X)]
- Propensity scores are clipped to avoid extreme weights

17. Measurement error diagnostic
- Uses Ỹ (LLM stance from Part B) as the outcome
- Checks: Corr(Ỹ, D) — if non-zero, measurement error is non-classical
- Non-classical error means the LLM proxy is systematically correlated
  with treatment assignment, violating standard assumptions
- Compares naive LLM ATE vs. adjusted LLM ATE (controlling for year)
- The gap between them reflects the confounding that year introduces

18. Key message
- The naive estimator conflates the ATT with selection bias
- Conditioning on X (year) removes confounding under ignorability
- The G-formula and IPW give consistent estimates of the ATE
- LLM-derived outcomes exhibit non-classical measurement error:
  the proxy Ỹ is correlated with D, so naive LLM estimates are biased
  even after controlling for observed covariates

CORE TAKEAWAY
--------------------------------------------------

"In Part A, the outcome was arbitrary—text length—and we obtained a large difference.

In Part B, we construct the outcome from text using an LLM, and the estimated effect
is completely different.

In Part C, we apply the full identification framework: we verify overlap, remove
confounding via the adjustment formula and IPW, and show that the LLM-derived
outcome exhibits non-classical measurement error—its values are systematically
correlated with treatment assignment, so naive LLM estimates are biased in a way
that does not disappear with larger samples.

This shows that causal conclusions depend critically on both how the outcome is
measured and whether the identification assumptions are satisfied."
